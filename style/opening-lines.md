# Opening Lines

The first sentence is a contract. It tells the reader: this is the voice, this is the world, this is what's at stake. A good opening doesn't just begin the story — it *is* the story in miniature.

## What an Opening Must Do

At least one of these:

- **Establish voice** — the reader hears the narrator and decides to trust (or distrust) them
- **Create a question** — something is wrong, unresolved, or unexplained
- **Set the terms** — the rules of this world, this story
- **Begin in motion** — something is already happening

The strongest openings do several simultaneously.

## The Openings, Examined

### The World-Reframing Declaration

> "It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife." — Austen, *Pride and Prejudice*

Presents a social obsession as universal law. The irony is in "universally acknowledged" — this is *one class's* fixation stated with the confidence of natural law. The voice — amused, precise, knowingly distant — is the entire novel's instrument. The reader hears Austen's mind and decides to stay.

### The Impossible Fact

> "As Gregor Samsa awoke one morning from uneasy dreams he found himself transformed in his bed into a gigantic insect." — Kafka, *The Metamorphosis*

States the impossible in the flattest possible prose. No buildup, no "he couldn't believe his eyes." The horror isn't the transformation — it's the *tone*. Kafka treats waking up as a bug with the same register as waking up late for work. The gap between the extraordinary event and the ordinary voice *is* the story's meaning.

### The Temporal Collapse

> "Many years later, as he faced the firing squad, Colonel Aureliano Buendía was to remember that distant afternoon when his father took him to discover ice." — García Márquez, *One Hundred Years of Solitude*

Three timelines in one sentence — the firing squad, the remembering, the discovery of ice. The juxtaposition of execution and childhood wonder tells you this novel treats time as elastic and death as ordinary. The word "discover" applied to *ice* tells you this is a world where the mundane is miraculous.

### The Voice That Seduces

> "Lolita, light of my life, fire of my loins. My sin, my soul. Lo-lee-ta: the tip of the tongue taking a trip of three steps down the palate to tap, at three, on the teeth." — Nabokov, *Lolita*

The sound is the seduction. The repetition of L's and T's — the tongue literally tapping — makes Humbert's obsession *audible*. It's gorgeous prose in service of a monstrous narrator. The contradiction "my sin, my soul" — positive and negative in every phrase — warns the reader: this beauty is the trap. Nabokov himself follows this with: "You can always count on a murderer for a fancy prose style." The narrator *knows* he's performing. The novel's central problem — aesthetic seduction versus moral horror — is embedded in three sentences.

### The Subtle Wrongness

> "It was a bright cold day in April, and the clocks were striking thirteen." — Orwell, *Nineteen Eighty-Four*

Normal until the last word. Clocks striking thirteen is *one digit off*. Not fantastically wrong, just subtly wrong. That slight displacement is totalitarianism's texture — recognizable enough to feel real, distorted enough to feel trapped.

### The Confessional Refusal

> "If you really want to hear about it, the first thing you'll probably want to know is where I was born, and what my lousy childhood was like, and all that David Copperfield kind of crap, but I don't feel like going into it, if you want to know the truth." — Salinger, *The Catcher in the Rye*

Character through *refusal*. Holden tells you he won't tell you — and tells you everything in the process. The run-on structure, the casual profanity, Dickens dismissed as "crap," the tic phrase "if you want to know the truth" (revealing that truth is exactly what Holden can't access) — all voice.

### The Emotional Void

> "Mother died today. Or maybe yesterday; I can't be sure." — Camus, *The Stranger*

A death reported with the emotional register of a weather update. The uncertainty — today or yesterday — isn't confusion, it's *indifference*. Two sentences and the reader knows Meursault's defining trait: he doesn't feel what he's supposed to feel.

### The Kidnapping

> "124 was spiteful. Full of a baby's venom." — Morrison, *Beloved*

Morrison described wanting "the reader to be kidnapped, thrown ruthlessly into an alien environment as the first step into a shared experience with the book's population — just as the slaves were from one place to another, without preparation or defense." She gives numerals where the reader expects words. She gives "spiteful" to a number. She separates the second sentence from the first — "Full of a baby's venom" — as its own phrase to put the stress on *full*. The missing "3" in the address 1-2-4 represents the murdered third child. Every choice is deliberate. Nothing is explained for seventeen lines.

### The Gothic Echo

> "Last night I dreamt I went to Manderley again." — du Maurier, *Rebecca*

"Last night" — the narrator speaks from after. "Dreamt" — Manderley exists only in memory. "Again" — a recurring dream, a place she can't stop returning to. The story is already over, the house already gone. The gothic atmosphere arrives before any gothic event.

### The Universal Claim

> "All happy families are alike; each unhappy family is unhappy in its own way." — Tolstoy, *Anna Karenina*

An artistic manifesto disguised as observation. Tolstoy is telling you: happiness is uninteresting; unhappiness has specificity. It's also quietly untrue (happy families are not all alike), and that productive untruth invites the reader to argue — which is engagement.

## What Bad Openings Do

**Weather without purpose.** Weather as filler. Weather works only when it's *doing* something — Orwell's April coldness, Jackson's June sunshine before murder.

**Throat-clearing.** "In order to understand what happened next..." The opening should *be* the beginning, not announce its approach.

**The alarm clock.** A character waking up is the fiction equivalent of a loading screen. Start when something is already happening.

**Over-promising.** "Nothing would ever be the same again." Hollow drama. The reader hasn't invested, so the stakes are zero.

## Diagnostic Questions

1. Does this sentence make the reader ask a question?
2. Can I hear the voice? Would I recognize this narrator in a lineup?
3. Is something already in motion, or am I still setting up?
4. Does this opening contain the story's DNA — its themes, its way of seeing?
5. If this were the only sentence a reader saw, would they keep going?
