# Economy

Good writing is not writing that adds until it's complete. It's writing that cuts until what remains is essential. Every word must earn its place. This isn't minimalism as style — it's respect for the reader's attention.

## The Principle

Chekhov told his brother six rules for fiction. The fifth: "Extreme brevity."

He also wrote, in his own formulation of the gun principle: "Remove everything that has no relevance to the story. If you say in the first chapter that there is a rifle hanging on the wall, in the second or third chapter it absolutely must go off. If it's not going to be fired, it shouldn't be hanging there."

Every detail makes a promise. A detailed description of a red door tells the reader the red door matters. If it doesn't, the detail is a lie.

Mark Twain: "I didn't have time to write you a short letter, so I wrote you a long one." Brevity is harder than length.

## What Economy Looks Like

### Hemingway: Every Word Under Oath

From "A Clean, Well-Lighted Place":

> It was very late and everyone had left the café except an old man who sat in the shadow the leaves of the tree made against the electric light.

One sentence. A time, a place, a character, a mood, an image. "Shadow the leaves of the tree made" — not "the shadow cast by the leaves." The compressed phrasing mirrors the specificity of light through leaves.

From *The Old Man and the Sea*:

> He was an old man who fished alone in a skiff in the Gulf Stream and he had gone eighty-four days now without taking a fish.

Every clause adds one fact. Old. Alone. Skiff. Gulf Stream. Eighty-four days. No fish. Forty-one words. The sentence is a ramp — each detail raises the stakes.

Hemingway's style grew directly from his iceberg theory and his journalism training. As one analysis of "Hills Like White Elephants" noted, his dialogue uses "basic and common words with specific meaning and few syllables. Nouns are often used rather than adjectives, verbs rather than adverbs." The story's 144 lines of dialogue are all direct speech — no authorial intrusion, no emotional stage directions.

### Carver: The Art of Leaving Out

From "What We Talk About When We Talk About Love":

> The gin and the tonic water kept going around, and we somehow got on the subject of love.

"Somehow" is doing immense work. It tells you these characters don't know how they got here — into this conversation, into their lives. One adverb carries the weight of four characters' emotional avoidance.

### Orwell: The Window Pane

From "Politics and the English Language":

> Good prose is like a window pane.

Then his rules:
1. Never use a metaphor you are used to seeing in print.
2. Never use a long word where a short one will do.
3. If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out.
4. Never use the passive where you can use the active.
5. Never use a foreign phrase or jargon word if you can think of an everyday English equivalent.
6. Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.

Rule six is the most important. The rules serve clarity. When clarity requires breaking them, break them.

## What to Cut

**Qualifiers.** "Very," "really," "quite," "somewhat," "slightly." These are the writer hedging. If "tired" isn't strong enough, the problem isn't the missing intensifier — it's the wrong word. Try "spent" or "hollowed out."

**Redundancies.** "She nodded her head." What else would she nod? "They gathered together." Cut the anatomical footnotes.

**Warm-up sentences.** First drafts often start a paragraph one sentence too early. The writer finds their way in. The reader doesn't need the runway. Cut the first sentence and see if the second was the actual beginning.

**Explaining the metaphor.** "The city was a furnace, hot and relentless and unforgiving." Trust the metaphor. "The city was a furnace." Done.

**Stage directions.** "He reached out his hand and picked up the glass and brought it to his lips and drank." → "He drank." Unless the reaching matters.

**Throat-clearing phrases.** "It should be noted that," "It is interesting that," "As a matter of fact," "In order to." The prose equivalent of "um."

## Economy Is Not Minimalism

Economy doesn't mean short sentences and simple words. It means *no waste*.

Faulkner's sentences run for pages, but at their best, every clause earns its place — the accumulation *is* the meaning. Morrison's prose is lush and layered but nothing is decorative. Nabokov's *Speak, Memory*: "The cradle rocks above an abyss, and common sense tells us that our existence is but a brief crack of light between two eternities of darkness." That's baroque. But every word — "cradle," "rocks," "abyss," "crack," "eternities" — is load-bearing. Remove any word and the architecture weakens.

The question is not "how short can I make this?" The question is "is everything here doing something?"

## The Revision Test

Read each sentence and ask: **what happens if I delete this?**

- If nothing is lost → delete it.
- If the meaning survives but the rhythm breaks → keep it. It's doing musical work.
- If the meaning changes → it's load-bearing. Keep it.
- If it's doing what the previous sentence already did → one of them goes.

Then read each *word* in the surviving sentences and ask the same question.

## Diagnostic Questions

1. Read the paragraph aloud. Where do you get bored? Cut there.
2. What's the first sentence that *matters*? Is it the first sentence?
3. Can I say this in half the words? Try it. If nothing is lost, the shorter version wins.
4. Am I explaining something the reader already understands?
5. Is any word here for *me* (because I like the sound) rather than for the *reader* (because it serves the meaning)?
