# Story Frameworks

Every popular story framework is a different lens on the same underlying shape. Learning several is useful not because you should pick one and follow it, but because each makes a different part of the structure legible. Use them as diagnostic tools, not templates.

## Freytag's Pyramid (Five Acts)

Gustav Freytag, writing in 1863, mapped classical tragedy onto a pyramid: **exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, denouement.** The climax sits in the *middle*, not near the end.

This is the structure of *Oedipus Rex*, *Hamlet*, *Macbeth* — tragedies where the turning point is a decision made mid-story, and the rest of the play is the consequences unfolding. Once Macbeth kills Duncan, the remaining three acts are the slow detonation.

**When this framework helps:** tragedy, or any story where the protagonist's fatal choice happens early and the rest of the novel is fallout. The "midpoint reversal" in modern three-act structure is a ghost of Freytag's central climax — relocated from act 3 to mid-act 2, but doing the same work.

**Its weakness:** the falling action drags for most modern readers, who are trained on a climax placed late. Contemporary novels almost never use true Freytag.

## Hero's Journey (Campbell / Vogler)

Joseph Campbell's *The Hero with a Thousand Faces* (1949) identified a recurring pattern in world mythology: the **monomyth**, 17 stages of departure, initiation, and return. Christopher Vogler condensed it to 12 stages for screenwriters.

The 12 stages: Ordinary World, Call to Adventure, Refusal of the Call, Meeting the Mentor, Crossing the Threshold, Tests/Allies/Enemies, Approach to the Inmost Cave, Ordeal, Reward, The Road Back, Resurrection, Return with the Elixir.

This maps cleanly onto three acts: Ordinary World through Crossing the Threshold is Act I; Tests through Reward is Act II; Road Back through Return is Act III. Vogler's contribution was showing that the "mythic" shape and the commercial three-act shape are the *same thing*.

**When this framework helps:** stories of transformation — the protagonist is changed by the journey. Epic fantasy, coming-of-age, quest narratives. Star Wars, The Lord of the Rings, Harry Potter all track it nearly beat-for-beat.

**Its weakness:** it's a character-arc framework dressed as a plot framework. It tells you a lot about who the protagonist should become and little about what should actually happen on page 200. Pair it with another framework for event-level planning.

## Save the Cat (Blake Snyder, 15 Beats)

Blake Snyder's 2005 screenwriting book *Save the Cat!* broke the three-act structure into 15 beats with specific page targets. Novelist Jessica Brody adapted it for fiction in *Save the Cat! Writes a Novel.*

The 15 beats, in order: Opening Image, Theme Stated, Setup, Catalyst, Debate, Break into Two, B Story, Fun and Games, Midpoint, Bad Guys Close In, All Is Lost, Dark Night of the Soul, Break into Three, Finale, Final Image.

Most of these map to the three-act beats: *Catalyst* is the inciting incident, *Break into Two* is the first plot point, *All Is Lost* is the end of Act II, and so on. The additions worth stealing even if you don't use the rest:

- **Opening Image / Final Image** — bookend the novel with a matched pair of images that show how much the protagonist has changed.
- **Theme Stated** — somewhere in Act I, a minor character says the thing the novel is actually about. The protagonist dismisses or misunderstands it. They'll come back to it.
- **B Story** — a subplot (often the love story, or the mentor relationship) that carries the *theme* while the A plot carries the *action*. The B story is where the protagonist's internal change happens.
- **Fun and Games** — the first half of Act II should deliver on the *premise* — the reason the reader picked up the book. A vampire novel's Fun and Games is vampires. A heist novel's is the heist prep.

**When this framework helps:** commercial fiction where pacing is paramount. Thrillers, romance, YA, genre. Also excellent for diagnosing a draft: if you can't identify which scene is which beat, the structure is vague.

**Its weakness:** if applied rigidly, the pacing becomes uniform — every novel hitting every beat at the same percentage. Critics of "Save the Cat culture" argue it's produced a generation of films and novels that feel mechanically identical.

## The Seven-Point Structure (Dan Wells)

Dan Wells, adapting a game-design framework, teaches seven points: **Hook, Plot Turn 1, Pinch Point 1, Midpoint, Pinch Point 2, Plot Turn 2, Resolution.** He recommends writing them in a specific order: endpoints first (Hook and Resolution), then the Midpoint, then the Plot Turns, then the Pinch Points.

The order matters. If you know where you start and end, you know what the midpoint has to accomplish (get the character from one to the other). If you know the midpoint, you know what the plot turns have to set up.

**The pinch points** — roughly 37% and 62% — are moments where the antagonist's pressure is applied directly. The stakes become visible. Sauron's eye turns toward Frodo. The shark appears.

**When this framework helps:** as a planning tool for writers who find Save the Cat's 15 beats too granular and three-act too vague. The write-the-endpoints-first method is useful regardless of which framework you ultimately use.

## Kishōtenketsu (Four-Act, No Conflict)

The dominant narrative structure in classical Chinese, Korean, and Japanese literature. Four parts: **Ki (introduction), Shō (development), Ten (twist), Ketsu (conclusion).**

The key departure from Western frameworks: **no central conflict is required.** The story doesn't need a protagonist fighting an antagonist. Instead, meaning emerges from *contrast* — the twist (Ten) recontextualizes the first two acts, and the conclusion (Ketsu) reconciles the old and new perspectives.

A classic example: a woman is walking with her daughter. The daughter picks a flower. The mother remembers her own mother picking that same flower. The story is complete. Nothing was fought for, nothing overcome — and yet meaning has been generated by the juxtaposition of generations.

Miyazaki's films often run on Kishōtenketsu logic. *My Neighbor Totoro* has no villain. The structure is: children move to the country (Ki), they encounter forest spirits (Shō), the younger sister goes missing (Ten), she's found (Ketsu). Conflict exists but isn't structural.

**When this framework helps:** literary fiction, mood pieces, slice-of-life, and any story where the Western conflict-driven shape feels forced onto material that doesn't want it. Also useful as a corrective — if every scene in your novel is characters fighting each other, you may have mistaken "conflict" for "tension." They are not the same (see `structure/tension`).

**Its weakness:** Western readers — especially in commercial markets — are trained to expect confrontation. A pure Kishōtenketsu novel can read as "nothing happens" to a reader waiting for the climax. Hybrid approaches are common.

## Which to Use

None. Or all of them. Frameworks are not competing products — they're overlapping maps of the same territory.

A practical approach:

1. **Plan** with whichever framework matches your instinct for the project. Epic fantasy: Hero's Journey. Thriller: Save the Cat. Literary: Kishōtenketsu or loose three-act. Tragedy: Freytag.
2. **Draft** without looking at the framework. Structure-as-straitjacket kills prose. The plan is there to stop you panicking, not to dictate sentences.
3. **Revise** by running the draft through a *different* framework than you planned with. If you planned Save the Cat, run the draft against Hero's Journey. The mismatches reveal structural weaknesses that your original framework had blind spots for.

The goal is internalized structural intuition — the point where you no longer consult the framework because you can feel when a scene is load-bearing and when it isn't. The frameworks are training wheels for that intuition, not substitutes.

## Common Failure Modes

**Framework worship.** Treating the framework as prescriptive. A novel that hits every Save the Cat beat on schedule but has no life on the page is a dead novel.

**Framework shopping.** Switching frameworks mid-draft looking for the one that will "unlock" the book. The problem is almost never the framework.

**Framework denial.** Insisting one's novel is "beyond structure." Almost always, the novel has structure — just unintentional, and usually bad.

**Mistaking structure for plot.** Two novels can share a structural skeleton and feel nothing alike. Structure is where the weight sits. What the reader actually experiences is the prose, the characters, and the specifics. Structure supports those. It does not replace them.
