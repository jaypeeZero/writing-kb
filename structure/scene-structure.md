# Scene Structure

A scene is the smallest unit of story that does work. If nothing changes between the opening and the close, the scene can be cut.

## What a Scene Needs

### 1. Goal
The POV character wants something. It can be small (find shelter, get information, avoid embarrassment) but it must be specific. A character without a want is a camera. Vague goals produce vague scenes.

### 2. Resistance
Something opposes the goal. Another character's agenda, a physical obstacle, an internal conflict, a ticking clock. Without friction, the scene has no engine. Two characters who agree with each other and cooperate pleasantly are not in a scene — they're in an information exchange.

### 3. Turn
The character's situation, understanding, or options must be different at the end of the scene than at the beginning. This is non-negotiable. The turn can come from:

- **Action** — something happens that changes the value (safety → danger, hope → doubt, ignorance → knowledge)
- **Revelation** — new information reframes what the character thought was true

If the opening state and closing state are the same, nothing happened.

### 4. Outcome That Causes the Next Scene
The scene's result — a failure, a new problem, an unanswered question, a cost — becomes the input for the next scene. This causal chain is plot. If you can rearrange your scenes in any order without breaking the story, they aren't linked by cause and effect.

## How a Scene Moves

Internally, a scene escalates through **beats** — small exchanges of action and reaction that build pressure. Each beat raises the stakes or narrows the character's options. Beats should not repeat at the same intensity. If three consecutive beats are at the same level of tension, the scene is stalling.

**The general shape:**
1. Establish the goal and the starting state
2. Introduce resistance
3. Escalate — complications get worse, options narrow, the initial strategy fails
4. Crisis — the character faces a choice (ideally between two bad options, or two good options that are mutually exclusive)
5. Climax — the character acts on that choice, revealing who they are under pressure
6. Resolution — the consequences land, and the new state is clear

Not every scene needs all six at full intensity. A quiet scene can compress the escalation. A children's book scene can have gentle stakes. But the *shape* — pressure building toward a choice that changes something — is what makes a scene move rather than sit.

## Scenes End in Trouble

The natural ending for a scene is not success. If the character gets what they want, the reader has no reason to continue. Effective scene endings:

- The character fails to get what they wanted
- The character gets what they wanted but it costs something
- The character gets what they wanted and discovers it wasn't what they needed
- A new, worse problem emerges from the attempt
- The question the scene raised remains unanswered

This doesn't mean every scene ends in catastrophe. It means every scene ends with forward tension — something unresolved that pulls the reader into the next scene.

## The Crucible

Characters in a scene must be *held together*. They can't just walk away. Either the physical situation traps them, or their motivation to stay (desire, duty, pride, curiosity) is stronger than their motivation to leave. If a character can exit without cost, the scene has no container for its pressure.

## Subtext

The best scenes have a gap between what characters say and what they mean. Characters with hidden agendas, unspoken fears, or conflicting loyalties create subtext naturally. If everyone says exactly what they think and means exactly what they say, the scene will feel flat regardless of what's happening in the plot.

Dialogue is not information exchange — it's characters trying to get what they want from each other. Each character should have a different agenda in the conversation. When their agendas collide, you have conflict without anyone raising their voice.

## Common Scene Failures

**No goal.** The character wanders into the scene without wanting anything. Things happen around them. They observe. They leave. This produces travelogue, not story.

**No turn.** The character enters and exits in the same state. The scene was pleasant or interesting but didn't change anything. Ask: what is different now?

**Conflict without stakes.** Characters argue or face obstacles, but nothing is at risk. The outcome doesn't matter to anyone, including the reader.

**The character succeeds too easily.** Goal → achievement, no friction. The scene is over before it started. Even in a quiet story, the character should have to work for it — adjust, reconsider, try a different angle.

**Summary instead of scene.** Events are reported rather than experienced in real time. If you can't picture it happening moment by moment — if it reads like a recap — it's summary wearing a scene's clothes.

**Two scenes in a trench coat.** Two distinct encounters or events crammed into one chapter without either fully developing. Each scene needs room to establish, escalate, and turn. If a chapter has two separate conflicts, it probably wants to be two scenes.

**Redundant scenes.** Two scenes that make the same point. A character is told "you carry too much" in one scene and then told "you carry too much" in the next. The second scene needs to escalate — new angle, higher stakes, a cost the first scene didn't have — or it's doing the same work twice.

## Diagnostic Questions

When a scene feels off, ask:

1. What does the character want in this scene?
2. What's stopping them from getting it?
3. What's different at the end than at the beginning?
4. Why can't the character just leave?
5. What does this scene's outcome force to happen next?
6. Is there a gap between what characters say and what they mean?

If you can't answer the first three, the scene doesn't have its bones yet.
