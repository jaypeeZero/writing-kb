# Pacing

Pacing is the writer's control of time. Not clock time — *experienced* time. A year can pass in a sentence. A single second can take three pages. The reader's sense of speed is controlled entirely by how the writer deploys expansion and compression.

## The Two Gears

**Scene** — real-time dramatization. Dialogue, action, sensory detail. Time slows or matches reality. This is where the story *lives*.

**Summary** — compressed narration. Days, weeks, years pass in a paragraph. The writer tells rather than shows. This is how the story *moves*.

The art is in choosing which moments get scene and which get summary. The rule: **give scene time to the moments that change things.** Summarize transitions and routines. If you're writing a scene for something that could be a sentence, or summarizing something that deserves a scene, the pacing is wrong.

## Expansion: Slowing Time

When a moment matters, slow it down with *perceptual* detail. The character notices more because the moment is charged.

Tolstoy, *Anna Karenina* — Anna's suicide. Tolstoy gives every thought, every perception, every flicker of intention and revulsion. The train approaches. She watches the wheels. She makes the sign of the cross. Seconds in reality, pages on the page. The expansion *is* the horror — the reader is trapped inside Anna's consciousness at the worst possible moment.

McCarthy, *Blood Meridian* — violence is paced through expansion. A scalping that takes seconds gets a full paragraph. The prose *makes you stay*. That enforced attention is the point.

**What expansion requires:**
- Sensory specificity — not "the room" but the sound of the radiator, the smell of the carpet
- Interiority — what the character notices and can't look away from
- Micro-actions — a hand on a doorknob, a breath drawn, a glass set down
- White space — paragraphing that forces the reader to pause

## Compression: Accelerating Time

When you need to move, compress. Sentence length drops. Detail thins. Time telescopes.

García Márquez, *One Hundred Years of Solitude*:

> Colonel Aureliano Buendía organized thirty-two armed uprisings and he lost them all.

An entire military career in one sentence. The compression is the meaning — the futility of thirty-two attempts flattened into a single line. The reader doesn't need to see each uprising. The number does the emotional work.

Hemingway, "Indian Camp" — Nick just witnessed a cesarean with a jackknife and a suicide by razor. The aftermath:

> They walked back to the road. The doctor walked ahead. Nick looked back and saw a figure in the blanket by the fire. They walked a long way.

Four sentences. No interiority. No reflection. The compression *is* the emotional response: the inability to process, forward motion as coping mechanism.

**What compression requires:**
- Short sentences
- Omitted interiority — no time for thought, just action
- Telescoped time — "three weeks later," "by morning"
- Declarative verbs — subject-verb-object, no subordinate clauses

## Rhythm: The Alternation

A story paced at one speed is numbing. The rhythm comes from alternation — scene, summary, scene — with proportions shifting as stakes change.

Jackson, *The Haunting of Hill House* — long, hypnotic atmospheric passages, then a doorknob turning. The alternation creates breathing — inhale, exhale — that mirrors Eleanor's deterioration. The pacing *is* the horror.

**The general shape:**

1. **Opening** — moderate pace. Enough scene to ground, enough summary to move.
2. **Rising action** — scenes lengthen. Moments that matter are getting closer together.
3. **Climax** — maximum expansion. The decisive moment gets the most time.
4. **Resolution** — compression returns. The aftermath is summary. The story exhales.

This isn't formula — it's tendency. Some stories invert it. But the principle holds: **allocate page time in proportion to emotional weight.**

## Controlling Pace Implicitly

These tools change pace without requiring conscious pacing decisions:

**Paragraph length.** Short = fast. Long = slow. One-sentence paragraph = brakes.

**Sentence length.** Long sentences decelerate. Short sentences accelerate. Vary them.

**White space.** A line break is a beat of silence. Section breaks are longer pauses. Each creates a micro-moment where the reader surfaces.

**Dialogue vs. narration.** Dialogue is faster — the reader processes it in real time. A page of dialogue flies. A page of description crawls. Mix them.

**Detail density.** More specific detail = slower reading. The reader constructs each image. Strip the detail and the reader skims forward.

## The False Urgency Trap

Beginning writers mistake pacing for speed. Short sentences. Quick cuts. No breath. The result is exhausting and, paradoxically, boring — without expansion, nothing has weight. A story where everything is urgent is a story where nothing is.

The inverse is equally true: literary fiction sometimes mistakes slowness for depth. Meditative passages about light on a windowsill can be magnificent when they earn their place. When they don't, they're the writer indulging at the reader's expense.

## Diagnostic Questions

1. What moments in this story deserve the most page time? Are they getting it?
2. Where am I writing scene for something that should be summary?
3. Where am I summarizing something that deserves a scene?
4. Read a page aloud. Does the pace *feel* right for what's happening?
5. Is there variety? Or does every page read at the same speed?
6. Where does the reader need to breathe? Where should they be denied breath?
