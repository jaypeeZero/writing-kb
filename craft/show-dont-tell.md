# Show, Don't Tell

Chekhov wrote to his brother in 1886: "In descriptions of Nature one must seize on small details, grouping them so that when the reader closes his eyes he gets a picture. For instance, you'll have a moonlit night if you write that on the mill dam a piece of glass from a broken bottle glittered like a bright little star, and that the black shadow of a dog or a wolf rolled past like a ball."

That's the whole lesson. Don't announce the moonlight. Put the broken glass on the dam and let it glitter. Telling names the effect. Showing produces it.

## The Principle

Telling is the writer doing the reader's work: labeling an emotion, announcing a trait, declaring an atmosphere. Showing is the writer providing evidence and trusting the reader to reach the conclusion themselves. The reader who *arrives* at "she's grieving" feels it. The reader who's *told* "she was grief-stricken" registers it and moves on.

This doesn't mean never tell. It means: **tell for transitions, show for load-bearing moments.** When the emotion matters, when the character reveals who they are, when the scene must land — show.

## What Showing Looks Like

### Emotion Through Object and Action

Hemingway, "Hills Like White Elephants" — a story about abortion in which the word "abortion" never appears. The entire conflict lives in what characters do with beer glasses and how they look at hills:

> "They look like white elephants," she said.
> "I've never seen one," the man drank his beer.
> "No, you wouldn't have," she said.

Nobody says "she was bitter" or "he was dismissive." The bitterness lives in five words: "No, you wouldn't have." The man drinks his beer instead of engaging — avoidance made physical. The story has 144 lines of dialogue in direct speech with almost no narration. Hemingway removes himself entirely. Readers have to infer everything — the relationship, the power dynamic, the unspoken fear — from the surface conversation. The story is seven-eighths iceberg.

### Character Through Behavior

Flannery O'Connor, "A Good Man Is Hard to Find" — the grandmother spends the entire story performing goodness. She quotes morality. She laments that people aren't nice anymore. She calls Red Sammy "a good man" because he agrees with her opinions. Then, facing the Misfit's gun, she bargains for her own life while her family is taken into the woods.

O'Connor never writes "the grandmother was selfish" or "her Christianity was shallow." She shows a woman who hides her cat in a basket, lies about a plantation house to change the family's route, and causes the accident that kills them all. In the final moment — "Why you're one of my babies. You're one of my own children!" — O'Connor said the grandmother finally does "the right thing," one genuine involuntary gesture of grace. After a whole story of performed virtue, the only real moment is the one she doesn't control.

O'Connor herself explained: the grandmother "lacked comprehension, but... had a good heart." We don't need O'Connor to tell us that. The story shows it.

### Grief Without Naming It

Joan Didion, *The Year of Magical Thinking*:

> I could not give away the rest of his shoes. I stood there for a moment, then realized why: he would need shoes if he was to return.

The word "grief" doesn't appear. The irrationality of the thought — keeping shoes for a dead man because the alternative is unthinkable — *is* the grief. Didion doesn't describe her pain. She describes the shoes. The reader fills in everything else.

Raymond Carver, "A Small, Good Thing" — a couple's child dies. The baker, who doesn't know, keeps calling about an uncollected birthday cake. When they finally confront him, he says:

> "Let me say how sorry I am," the baker said, putting his elbows on the table. "God alone knows how sorry."

Then he feeds them. Warm rolls, in the middle of the night. Carver doesn't write about grief resolving. He writes about eating bread at 3 AM. The concrete detail carries the emotional weight.

### Atmosphere Through Specific Detail

Shirley Jackson, "The Lottery" — opens with cheerful pastoral description: clear sunny morning, flowers blossoming, grass richly green. Children gather stones. Nobody explains why. The pleasant details become sinister retroactively. Jackson never tells you to be afraid. The horror lands harder because the prose insists everything is normal.

## The Telling Trap

Telling intrudes most often in these forms:

**Emotion labels.** "He was furious." Replace with what fury looks like in *this* character. Does he go quiet? Clean the kitchen? Smile? The specific expression *is* the character.

**Trait announcements.** "She was the kind of person who always helped others." This is a résumé. Show her helping. Better: show her helping when it costs her something.

**Atmospheric adjectives.** "The room was eerie and unsettling." What makes it eerie? The specific detail does the atmospheric work. Jackson proved this — sunny and pleasant is more unsettling than gothic when the context is right.

**Reader instructions.** "Little did she know, this would change everything." If the scene works, the reader knows. If it doesn't, the announcement won't save it.

## When Telling Works

**Transitions.** "Three weeks passed." You cannot and should not dramatize everything.

**Established rhythm.** Once a trait has been shown, subsequent references can tell. Chekhov shows his characters in their first scene, then uses summary to move through time. The reader's initial image does the work.

**Velocity.** In high-action sequences, telling can accelerate. McCarthy's chase scenes telescope into summary because slowing down would kill the pace.

**Ironic distance.** Austen's narration tells constantly — "It is a truth universally acknowledged..." — but the telling is the author's voice performing. The gap between the telling and the truth is the comedy.

**Chekhov himself.** The man who gave us the principle didn't follow it rigidly. In "The Lady with the Dog," he writes both showing ("At home in Moscow everything was in its winter routine; the stoves were heated") and telling ("It seemed to him that he had been so instructed by bitter experience"). He was a great writer, not a rulebook. The principle serves the story, not the other way around.

## The Iceberg

Hemingway's iceberg theory: the dignity of movement of an iceberg is due to only one-eighth of it being above water. The writer knows everything. The reader sees the surface — the objects, the gestures, the words. What's beneath is *felt* precisely because it's not stated.

In "Hills Like White Elephants," you never learn the characters' real names, whether they stay together, or what they decide. You get two people, two beers, some hills, and a conversation that circles an abyss without ever naming it. As one critic noted, Hemingway "completely removes himself from the story. Readers are never aware of an author's voice behind the story." That's showing at its most radical: the writer disappears entirely.

## Diagnostic Questions

1. Am I naming an emotion? What would its *evidence* look like in this character?
2. Am I telling the reader how to feel about a scene? What if I removed that instruction?
3. Can the reader reach this conclusion from what I've shown? If yes, the telling is redundant.
4. Is this a load-bearing moment? If so, show. Summary won't hold the weight.
5. Have I trusted the image? If the concrete detail is right, the abstraction is unnecessary.
