# Dialogue

Dialogue is not characters talking. It is characters *doing things to each other with words*. Every line of dialogue is an action — a attempt to persuade, deflect, wound, seduce, evade, or control. If a line doesn't have a verb of intention behind it, it's furniture.

## What Dialogue Does

Dialogue must do at least two things simultaneously. Any single line can:

- **Reveal character** — not through self-description ("I'm the kind of person who...") but through word choice, rhythm, what's avoided, what's deflected
- **Advance the conflict** — shift the power dynamic, raise the stakes, close off an option
- **Create subtext** — establish a gap between what's said and what's meant
- **Deliver information** — but only when the information is *contested*, *reluctant*, or *costs something* to say

A line that does only one of these is weak. A line that does none can be cut.

## Subtext

The best dialogue lives in the gap between surface and meaning. Characters rarely say what they actually want. They circle it, test for it, protect themselves from the vulnerability of stating it directly.

Subtext emerges naturally when characters have:
- **Something to hide** — a secret, a fear, a desire they won't admit
- **Unequal knowledge** — one character knows something the other doesn't, and the reader can feel the asymmetry
- **Social constraint** — politeness, rank, audience, or taboo prevents direct speech

A character who says "I'm fine" when they are clearly not fine is the simplest form of subtext. A character who talks about the weather while deciding whether to leave their marriage is a better one. The reader's pleasure comes from reading between the lines — from understanding more than the words alone convey.

**The test:** If you can replace dialogue with direct statement and lose nothing, the scene has no subtext. "I hate you" is information. "Don't forget your coat" — said by a character who's been left — is subtext.

## Each Line Is a Move

Think of dialogue as a game where each character has an objective. They don't take turns sharing information — they take turns *trying to get what they want*. Each line is a tactic:

- **Deflection** — answering a question with a different question
- **Attack** — shifting blame, raising an old wound, going on the offensive
- **Retreat** — conceding ground to protect something more important
- **Seduction** — offering something the other character wants in exchange for compliance
- **Evasion** — technically answering without revealing anything

When both characters want something and neither will yield, dialogue generates its own energy. When characters cooperate pleasantly and exchange information, the scene dies. Two characters who agree with each other are not in a scene.

## Compression

Real speech is full of false starts, filler, repetition, and aimless circling. Fictional dialogue is *compressed*. It keeps the rhythm and texture of real speech while cutting everything that doesn't work.

What to cut:
- **Greetings and small talk** — unless the small talk *is* the scene (two people performing normalcy while something terrible sits between them)
- **Complete sentences** — people interrupt, trail off, leave thoughts unfinished. Grammatically perfect dialogue sounds written
- **Answering the question that was asked** — in real conflict, people answer the question they *wish* had been asked, or the question behind the question
- **Stage directions in speech** — "Well, I think we should go to the store and buy some milk and then come back" is narration wearing quotation marks

What to keep:
- The jagged rhythm of how people actually respond to each other
- The non sequiturs that reveal a character is thinking about something else entirely
- The pauses, the unfinished sentences, the subject changes that signal discomfort

## Voice

Every character should be identifiable by their dialogue alone — not through accent spelling or catchphrases, but through *how they think*, which determines *how they speak*.

Voice comes from:
- **Vocabulary level** — a surgeon and a mechanic both describe precision, but with different words
- **Sentence structure** — some people speak in long, subordinate-clause-heavy sentences. Others don't. Some qualify everything. Others assert
- **What they notice** — a character's metaphors and references reveal their world. A farmer reaches for weather metaphors. A chess player sees moves and positions
- **Relationship to silence** — some characters fill every pause. Others weaponize quiet
- **Willingness to be direct** — some characters orbit a point. Others land on it hard

**The danger:** Dialect and phonetic spelling ("gonna," "shoulda," eye-dialect) creates the *appearance* of voice while often producing caricature. Voice lives in syntax and diction, not in apostrophes. Elmore Leonard could make every character in a room sound different without misspelling a word.

## Attribution

The word "said" is invisible. The reader's eye skips over it. This is a feature, not a bug. "Said" and "asked" do their work without drawing attention to themselves.

**Said bookisms** — "exclaimed," "retorted," "expostulated," "queried" — are the writer elbowing the reader. They tell the reader how to interpret the line instead of letting the line do its own work. If a line of dialogue needs "she said sarcastically" to land as sarcasm, the line has failed.

**Action beats** — small physical actions attached to dialogue — are more useful than attribution for two reasons: they identify the speaker *and* they keep the scene grounded in physical reality. "She set down her glass" between two lines of dialogue tells you who's speaking and where you are in space.

**When to drop attribution entirely:** In a rapid two-person exchange where the rhythm is established, you can run unattributed for several lines. The reader tracks by alternation. This speeds the pace and creates urgency. But re-anchor with attribution or a beat every four to five lines — the reader should never have to count backward to figure out who's speaking.

## Exposition

The deadliest dialogue sin is using characters as delivery systems for information the reader needs. "As you know, Bob, our father died three years ago and left us this house" is a writer talking to the reader through a character's mouth.

Exposition works in dialogue when:
- **The information is contested** — two characters disagree about the facts, so the exposition comes out through argument
- **The information costs something** — a character reveals something reluctantly, under pressure, and the revelation changes the dynamic
- **One character doesn't know** — a genuine knowledge gap (a new character, a child, someone who was absent) creates a natural reason to explain
- **The information is a weapon** — one character deploys facts to hurt, manipulate, or control another

The underlying principle: exposition in dialogue should be *dramatic*. If the information is inert — if nothing changes when it's spoken — it belongs in narration, not dialogue.

## Power and Status

Every conversation has a power dynamic. One character holds higher status — by knowledge, authority, emotional leverage, or simply by wanting less. The lower-status character works harder, talks more, justifies more, asks more questions.

Power shifts are the engine of great dialogue. The moment a subordinate reveals they know something the boss doesn't, the dynamic inverts. Watch for:
- **Who asks the questions** — the questioner usually holds power (interrogation) unless the questions are desperate (supplication)
- **Who controls the subject** — the character who can change the topic holds the conversation
- **Who can afford silence** — the character who can wait the other out has leverage
- **Who needs something** — need is vulnerability. The character who wants nothing is untouchable

## Silence and What's Not Said

Silence is dialogue's most powerful tool. What a character refuses to say — the question they don't answer, the accusation they don't deny, the "I love you" they don't return — creates more tension than any spoken line.

Use silence when:
- An answer would resolve tension you want to sustain
- The unspoken thing is more powerful than any words could be
- A character's refusal to engage *is* their answer

On the page, silence lives in beats ("He looked out the window"), in subject changes, and in responses that conspicuously fail to address what was just said.

## Common Dialogue Failures

**Identical voices.** Cover the character names. If you can't tell who's speaking, the characters don't have distinct voices. The fix isn't adding quirks — it's understanding each character's relationship to language.

**On-the-nose dialogue.** Characters say exactly what they feel. "I'm angry at you because you betrayed my trust." Real people almost never do this. They slam doors, change subjects, make cutting remarks about something unrelated. Emotion in dialogue should be *inferred*, not *stated*.

**Dialogue as information exchange.** Characters politely take turns conveying facts. No one has an agenda. No one resists. No one evades. This is an FAQ, not a scene.

**Everyone sounds like the writer.** All characters share the same vocabulary, the same sentence rhythms, the same cultural references. This is the most common failure and the hardest to fix because the writer can't hear it.

**Speechifying.** A character delivers a long, uninterrupted monologue in perfectly formed paragraphs. Real people don't speak in essays. Other characters interrupt, react, push back. If a character speaks for more than three or four sentences without being interrupted, there should be a dramatic reason for the silence of everyone else.

**Conflict without stakes.** Characters argue, but nothing is at risk. They can walk away, agree to disagree, or simply not care. Dialogue conflict only works when something the character values is on the line.

## From the Masters

### Hemingway — "Hills Like White Elephants"

The entire story is a dialogue about abortion without the word appearing once. 144 lines of direct speech, almost no narration. Hemingway removes himself entirely — no "she said bitterly," no "he spoke with forced calm."

> "It's really an awfully simple operation, Jig," the man said. "It's not really an operation at all."

The repetition of "really" is the tell — not redundancy but *insistence*. He names the operation then erases it in the same breath: "not really an operation at all." That's a tactical move: words as camouflage. Her silence that follows isn't passivity — it's her countermove. As one analysis put it, "every line spoken is an attempt. Every response is either resistance or retreat."

Later: "No, you wouldn't have," she said — five words carrying contempt, hurt, and the full inventory of a failing relationship. Hemingway proves that what characters *don't* say drives the scene.

### Carver — "What We Talk About When We Talk About Love"

> "The kind of love I'm talking about is. The kind of love I'm talking about, you don't try to kill people."

Broken syntax *is* the struggle. Terri can't finish her sentence. She starts, restarts, redefines. The repetition, the false start, the pivot to negation instead of definition — Carver doesn't describe confusion. The dialogue's rhythm performs it.

### Pinter — The Homecoming

Pinter's characters don't answer the question asked. They don't acknowledge what anyone else said. Five people in a room, five parallel monologues sharing airspace. The disconnection *is* the meaning. Pinter proved that families don't communicate — they broadcast at each other.

## Diagnostic Questions

When dialogue feels flat, ask:

1. What does each character want in this conversation?
2. What is each character unwilling to say?
3. If I cover the names, can I tell who's speaking?
4. Is there a gap between what's said and what's meant?
5. Does the power dynamic shift during the exchange?
6. Could I cut this conversation and lose nothing from the story?
7. Is any character saying exactly what they think and feel? Why are they that exposed?

If you can't answer the first two, the dialogue has no engine.
